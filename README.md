## II. Mathematical Foundation: The Prediction-Error Space PE(M)

### 2.1 The Prediction-Error Space as Topological Object

Every cognitive system — a developing child processing its caregiver's contingent responses, an adult scholar navigating institutional paradigms, a knowledge institution managing its internal consistency — operates on a cognitive manifold M. As established by CNIT and DIMT, M is a finite-dimensional vector space whose dimension is set by developmental history and bounded by genetic potential.

But M is not the object on which curiosity and suppression act directly. Curiosity is not about propositions in M; it is about the discrepancy between the current model encoded in M and the information that would update that model. The object of curiosity is a prediction error — the gap between what the model predicts and what the world provides.

GSDOS introduces the Prediction-Error Space PE(M) as the topological space of all such gaps. Formally, PE(M) is the tangent bundle T(M) of the cognitive manifold: the union of all tangent spaces at every point in M. Each element of PE(M) is a direction in M — a possible update to the model. An unresolved prediction error is a vector in T(M) at the current model position that points toward a better model but has not yet been followed.

The dimension of PE(M) is equal to dim(M) at each point, but the topology of PE(M) — which directions are accessible, which are blocked, which are richly connected — is what determines the character of a cognitive system's knowledge production.

Three properties of PE(M) determine intellectual output:

First, **size**: dim(PE(M)) = dim(M(A)), determined by the developmental operator and established in the critical window. This is the total number of prediction errors the system can in principle encounter.

Second, **accessibility**: the fraction of PE(M) that has accessible resolution paths — directions in which the model can actually move when it encounters a prediction error. This is κ, the curiosity coefficient, and it is the fraction of total prediction-error space for which the Genesis Operator has established a resolution pathway.

Third, **morphology**: the distribution of resolution difficulty across PE(M), encoded in ρ_PE. Some prediction errors are trivially resolvable (the child drops the block; it falls); some are moderately difficult (the tower falls in an unexpected direction; multiple hypotheses are possible); some are frontier-level (the mathematical theorem resists proof for decades). The morphology of PE(M) determines what kinds of resolution paths G must establish.

All prior frameworks modeled only the first property — size. GSDOS introduces the second and third, explaining the κ variance problem (identical Σ, different κ across institutions) and the Montessori delay (G must establish accessibility in PE(M) before the behavioral advantage is visible).

In GSDOS terms, each iteration of the PACE loop is one action of the Genesis Operator G: G takes a prediction error p and maps it to its resolution ρ(p) while simultaneously generating the next prediction error p' at the frontier of the updated model. G is not a one-shot operator; it is a recursive operator. Each application of G to a prediction error generates a new frontier of prediction errors. The geometric image of G's repeated action is a trajectory through prediction-error space that traces the boundary of the known model and continuously explores what lies beyond.


## V. The Duality Theorem and the φ-Fixed Point

### 5.1 The Commutator Formulation

**Theorem (Genesis-Suppressor Duality): The commutator [G, S] = GS − SG on the prediction-error space PE(M) has trace zero if and only if Σ = log φ ≈ 0.481.**

**Proof:**

G and S act on PE(M) as linear operators. Their commutator [G, S] = GS − SG measures the degree to which the order of their application matters: does applying G first and then S produce the same result as applying S first and then G?

When Σ < log φ (S-suboptimal, G-dominant): GS ≠ SG. Applying G first (opening prediction-error resolution paths) and then S (attempting to close them) produces fewer closures than applying S first and then G, because G's open paths are harder for S to close after they have been reinforced through dopaminergic consolidation. The commutator [G, S] is positive (G dominates).

The policy implication: sabbatical length should scale with the scholar's estimated developmental attention density. Institutions that provide uniform 6-month sabbaticals are optimizing for scholars from advantaged developmental backgrounds and systematically under-serving scholars whose creative contributions would require longer decompression.

### Thought Experiment 4: The Prediction-Error Budget of the Digital Platform Economy

Setup: The digital attention economy can be modeled as a Prediction-Error Budget manipulation. Major digital platforms (social media, short-video, algorithmic recommendation) resolve prediction errors before the user can pursue them through exploration. The G-operator requires that prediction errors be present AND accessible AND pursued through the full PACE cycle. Platform algorithms perform the Exploration stage on the user's behalf — they resolve what the user would have explored if given the opportunity.
